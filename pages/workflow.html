Preprocessing


DTM
Starting with the DTM obtained through GeoPortale Lombardia, we clipped the layer to our assigned zone. The resolution per pixel of this layer is 5 m/px, and based on this, we decided to also standardise the resolutions of the other layers to match this value. The values in this layer ranges from about 240 meters above the mean sea level (lower Val Cavallina) to a maximum of about 1400 meters, reached in the mountains surrounding the Endine Lake.




NDVI
Computed from ESA Sentinel 2 images, we clipped it from the entire Lombardy region to our case study zone and also resampled from 10 meters per pixel to 5 meters per pixel to match the choosen common resolution. The scale goes from 0 (lowest vegetation index, usually where there are buildings and roads) to 1 (highly vegetated areas).





DUSAF
Agricultural and Forestry Land Use Designation data from GeoPortale Lombardia, the territory is subdivided in classes based on the usage. Originally the starting layer was in WGS84 projection, so we reprojected it usingWarp (Reproject)function into WGS 84 / UTM zone 32N projection and then clipped as the previous layers to our study area. Also in this case we resampled the layer to 5 meters per pixel (original resolution 5.8 m/px).




Faults
Faults data from GeoPortale Lombardia, the territory is subdivided in classes based on the distance from the faults. After clipping to our study area as the previous layers, we converted it into raster usingRasterize (Vector to Raster)QGIS function since in this case the layer was vectorial.






Rivers
River network data retrieved from OpenStreetMap, the territory is subdivided in classes based on the distance from the rivers. After clipping to our study area as the previous layers, we converted it into raster usingRasterize (Vector to Raster)QGIS function since in this case the layer was vectorial.




Roads
Road network data retrieved from OpenStreetMap, the territory is subdivided in classes based on the distance from the roads. After clipping to our study area as the previous layers, we converted it into raster usingRasterize (Vector to Raster)QGIS function since in this case the layer was vectorial.





From the DTM layer several terrain attributes can be computed, for example slope, aspect and profile curvature. They are useful for evaluating the rate of change of slope and can be used to identify features like ridges and valleys in the terrain.


Aspect
Aspect computed from the DTM thanks to the GDAL - Aspect function in QGIS. The aspect is the orientation of the slope, and here it is subdivided in 8 classes for visualization purposes (N, NE, E, SE, S, SW, W, NW) plus flat areas.





Plan
Plan computed from the DTM thanks to the SAGA - Slope, Aspect, Curvature function in QGIS. The plan is perpendicular to the direction of the maximum slope. A positive value indicates the surface is sidewardly convex at that cell. A negative plan indicates the surface is sidewardly concave at that cell. A value of zero indicates the surface is linear Profile curvature relates to the convergence and divergence of flow across a surface.



Profile
Profile computed from the DTM thanks to the SAGA - Slope, Aspect, Curvature function in QGIS. The profile curvature is parallel to the direction of the maximum slope. A positive profile indicates that the surface is upwardly concave at that cell. A negative value indicates that the surface is upwardly convex at that cel. While a value of zero indicates that the surface is linear. The profile curvature affects the acceleration or deceleration of flow across the surface.


Slope
Slope computed from the DTM thanks to the GDAL - Slope function in QGIS. The slope refers to the rate of change of elevation for each cell within a digital elevation model (DEM). It is essentially the first derivative of a DEM, indicating how steep a particular part of the terrain is. The slope is usually expressed in degrees or as a percentage, representing the angle of incline or decline relative to a horizontal plane.


Then starting from Slope layer, we computed NLZ (No Landslides Zone), the zones in which a landslide theoretically can't occur. We obtained it using in the Raster calculator the following formula: "slope@1" < 20 OR "slope@1" > 70 and sieving it for remove too small areas, not significant for our purposes.
Moreover we used data from zone in which landslides have already occurred (from IFFI catalogue), both for removing that zones from computed NLZ as untrue information (since a landslide in that area has already happened) and also as example of area in which a landslide can occur. We did so in order to make our dataset more accurate for the next steps.

Susceptibility Map

To properly train and evaluate the model we generated a total of 1,000 points using the Random Points in layer bound tool, which randomly creates points within polygons. We assined the value 1 to the NLZ points and 2 to the LSZ points and we set a distance of 10 meters from one another. We decided to use a training-testing ratio of 70% for training and 30% for testing, maintaining a balanced 1:1 ratio in line with the 'Hazard' value. Then we performed a Merge operation on the training and testing layer, unifying them into one single layer.

Validation

Sequentially, to validate the output obtained previously, we used the Accuracy Assessment and Sampling, provided to us. We imported it into the processing scripts tools and ran it on the Susceptibility maps and testing points.
This is what we obtained:

Confusion Matrix

Class/Reference No Landslides Zone Landslides Zone User's Accuracy (%) Producer's Accuracy (%)
No Landslides Zone 121 39 0.76 0.81
Landslides Zone 29 111 0.79 0.74

  • Producer's accuracy (PA) for a class indicates the percentage that the class present in the ground truth is also captured by the classification.
    • PA = TP / (TP + FN)
  • User's accuracy (UA) for a class reveals how frequently a user of the classified map will encounter the class accurately representing the ground reality.
    • UA = TP / (TP + FP)
  • Overall accuracy (OA) represents the overall percentage of pixels that are correctly classified relative to the total number of pixels.
    • OA = (TP + TN) / (TP + TN + FP + FN)
The value of Overall Accuracy obtained is 0.773, being higher then 0.7, highlights a sufficiently high level of reliability in the results of the susceptibility map.

Exposure Assessment

To conduct the exposure assessment, we acquired, clipped and reprojected the population raster dataset to match the coordinate reference system (CRS) of the case-study area and the susceptibility map obtained in the preceding step.






Employing the Raster calculator, we categorized the susceptibility raster map into four distinct classes. This is the formula we used:
("susceptibility_map@1" < 25) * 1 +  
(("susceptibility_map@1" ≥ 25)AND("susceptibility_map@1" < 50)) * 2 +  
(("susceptibility_map@1" ≥ 50) AND ("susceptibility_map@1" < 75)) * 3 +  
("susceptibility_map@1" ≥ 75) * 4

  • Low: values in the range [0, 25) %
  • Moderate: values in the range [25, 50) %
  • High: values in the range [50, 75) %
  • Very high: values in the range [75, 100) %
To determine population exposure, a resampling procedure was conducted on the susceptibility map layer, ensuring its resolution conformed with that of the clipped population raster dataset, which is approximately 81.67 meters. By utilizing Raster layer zonal statistics, we computed the population count and lastly we used Excel to visualize the output through a pie chart, illustrating the proportion of population within each susceptibility class before defined.

The exposure assessment constitutes a crucial aspect in our project, indeed it aims at identifying and evaluating elements at risk within landslide susceptible zone. The principal aim of an exposure assessment is to quantify and comprehend the vulnerability of elements like the presence of individuals, buildings and infrastructure, facilitating the development of effective strategies for risk mitigation. In this exposure assessment, in particular, we are going to focus on the percentage of people that may be affected by potential landslide. By examinating the spatial correlation between areas prone to landslides and exposed elements, we can indentify high-risk zones and assess the potential repercussions of landslides on human activties.

Comparing the data obtained from our analysis with the percentage of population at risk of being affected by landslides in Italy we can see a staggering discrepancy, since the national percentage of population being at risk or at high risk according to ISPRA is 2.2% while the percentage of population resulting from our analysis at risk or high risk in the lake Endine surrounding area is around 6%.

Challenges Faced

These are some of the challenges that we encountered and how we overcame them:

  • We had to manually clip the NLZ layer into different sections in order to obtain better distributed points.
  • We used the GDAL tool (Aspect) instead of the SAGA (Slope,aspect,curvature), because otherwise it would indicate a NULL value in flat areas instead of zero, creating gaps within the layer and causing issues for subsequent operations.
  • We launched multiple times the tool to generate random points in order to produce points consistent with our study area.

WebGIS

In the final phase of our project, we have developed a WebGIS platform that facilitates the visualization of the analyzed maps and of the landslide susceptibility map. This tool is designed to be user-friendly, allowing individuals to easily interact with the map. Thanks to the intuitive interface, users are able to navigate through our collection of layers. They can effortlessly zoom in and out and switch between various layers making it possible to better comprehend the landslide susceptibility assessment. Moreover, our WebGIS platform is thoughtfully equipped with a set of base maps that serve as a backdrop, enhancing the overall user experience. The purpose of our WebGIS is to encourage users to explore and understand the data deeply, leading to a greater awareness of the nuances of landslide susceptibility and its potential impact on the environment. Additionally, our WebGIS is designed to be accessible to everyone, ensuring that users can gain valuable insights without the need for specialized technical knowledge.
