Preprocessing


DTM
Starting with the DTM obtained through GeoPortale Lombardia, we clipped the layer to our assigned zone. The resolution per pixel of this layer is 5 m/px, and based on this, we decided to also standardise the resolutions of the other layers to match this value. The values in this layer ranges from about 240 meters above the mean sea level (lower Val Cavallina) to a maximum of about 1400 meters, reached in the mountains surrounding the Endine Lake.




NDVI
Computed from ESA Sentinel 2 images, we clipped it from the entire Lombardy region to our case study zone and also resampled from 10 per pixel to 5 meters per pixel to match the choosen common resolution. The scale goes from 0 (lowest vegetation index, usually where there are buildings and roads) to 1 (healty vegetated areas).





DUSAF
Agricultural and Forestry Land Use Designation data from GeoPortale Lombardia, the territory is subdivided in classes based on the usage. Originally the starting layer was in WGS84 projection, so we reprojected it usingWarp (Reproject)function into WGS 84 / UTM zone 32N projection and then clipped as the previous layers to our study area. Also in this case we resampled the layer to 5 meters per pixel (original resolution 5.8 m/px).




Faults
Faults data from GeoPortale Lombardia, the territory is subdivided in classes based on the distance from the faults. After clipping to our study area as the previous layers, in this case the layer was vectorial, so we converted it into raster usingRasterize (Vector to Raster)QGIS function.






Rivers
River network data retrieved from OpenStreetMap, the territory is subdivided in classes based on the distance from the rivers. After clipping to our study area as the previous layers, in this case the layer was vectorial, so we converted it into raster usingRasterize (Vector to Raster)QGIS function.




Roads
Road network data retrieved from OpenStreetMap, the territory is subdivided in classes based on the distance from the roads. After clipping to our study area as the previous layers, in this case the layer was vectorial, so we converted it into raster usingRasterize (Vector to Raster)QGIS function.

Slope
Slope computed from the DTM thanks to the GDAL - Slope function in QGIS. The slope is the steepness of the terrain.







Then starting from Slope layer, we computed NLZ (No Landslides Zone), the zones in which a landslide theoretically can't occur. We obtained it using in the Raster calculator the following formula: "slope@1" < 20 OR "slope@1" > 70 and sieving for remove too small areas, not significant for our purposes.

Susceptibility Map

To properly train and evaluate the model we generated a total of 1,000 points using the Random Points in layer bound tool, which randomly allocated labels to polygons. We assined the value 1 to the NLZ and 2 to the LS and we set a distance of 10 meters from one another . We decided to use a training-testing ratio of 30% for training and 70% for testing, maintaining a balanced 1:1 ratio in line with the ‘Hazard’ value. Then we performed a Merge operation on the training and testing layer into two point layers: the Training Points and Testing points.

Susceptibility Map

To generate the susceptibility map we used the classification tool fram the Dzetsaka Plugin. We then converted the classification confidence to class probability using the raster calculator, using this formula
("classification@1" = 2) * "confidence@1" + ("classification@1" = 1) * (100 - "confidence@1")
Sequentially, to validate the output obtained previously, we used the Accuracy Assessment and Sampling, from the processing scripts tools, on the Susceptibility maps and testing point obtained from the previous computation.

  • Producer's accuracy (PA) for a class indicates the likelihood that the class present on the ground is also captured by the classification in the thematic raster.
    • PA = TP / (TP + FN)
  • User's accuracy (UA) for a class reveals how frequently a user of the classified map will encounter the class accurately representing the ground reality.
    • UA = TP / (TP + FP)
  • Overall accuracy (OA) represents the overall percentage of pixels that are correctly classified relative to the total number of pixels.
    • OA = (TP + TN) / (TP + TN + FP + FN)
The value of Overall Accuracy obtained is 0.773, being higher then 0.7, highlights a sufficiently high level of reliability in the results of the susceptibility map.

Exposure Assessment

To conduct the exposure assessment, we acquired and reprojected the population raster dataset to match the coordinate reference system (CRS) of the sub-area vector layer and the susceptibility map obtained in the preceding step.






Employing the Raster calculator, we categorized the susceptibility raster map into four distinct classes. This is the formula we used:
("susceptibility_map@1" < 25) * 1 +  
(("susceptibility_map@1" ≥ 25)AND("susceptibility_map@1" < 50)) * 2 +  
(("susceptibility_map@1" ≥ 50) AND ("susceptibility_map@1" < 75)) * 3 +  
("susceptibility_map@1" ≥ 75) * 4

  • Low: Values in the range [0, 25) %
  • Moderate: Values in the range [25, 50) %
  • High: Values in the range [50, 75) %
  • Very high: Values in the range [75, 100) %
To determine population exposure, a resampling procedure was conducted on the susceptibility map layer, ensuring its resolution conformed with that of the clipped population raster dataset, which approximated 81.67 meters. By utilizing Raster layer zonal statistics, we computed the population count and we subsequencially used Excel to visualize the output through a pie chart illustrating the proportion of population within each susceptibility class before defined.

The exposure assessment constitutes a crucial aspect in our assessment, indeed it aims at identifying and evaluating elements at risk within landslide susceptible zone. The principal aim of an exposure assessment is to quantify and comprehend the vulnerability of elements like the presence of individuals, of buildings, and infrastructure, facilitating the development of effective strategies for risk mitigation.In this exposure assessment in particular we are going to focus on the percentage of people that may be affected by potential landslide By examinating the spatial correlation between areas prone to landslides and exposed elements, we can indentify high-risk zones and assess the potential repercussions of landslides on human activties.

Challenges Faced

  • We had to manually clip the NLZ layer into different sections in order to obtain better distributed points.
  • We had to convert integer into floating-point to successfully merge the NLZ and LS-inventory layers.
  • We used the GDAL tool (Aspect) instead of the SAGA (Slope,aspect,curvature), because otherwise it would indicate a NULL value in flat areas instead of zero, creating gaps within the layer and causing issues for subsequent operations.
  • We launced multiple times the tool to generate random points in order to produce points consistent with our study area.

WebGIS

In the final phase of our project, we have developed a WebGIS platform that facilitates the visualization of the analyzed maps and of the landslide susceptibility map. This tool is designed to be user-friendly, allowing individuals to easily interact with the map. Thanks to the intuitive interface, users are able to navigate through our collection of layers. They can effortlessly zoom in and out and switch between various layers making it possible to better comprehend the landslide susceptibility assessment. Moreover, our WebGIS platform is thoughtfully equipped with a set of base maps that serve as a backdrop, enhancing the overall user experience. The overall scope of our webGis is to encourage users to explore and understand the data deeply, leading to a greater awareness of the nuances of landslide susceptibility and its potential impact on the environment. Additionally, our WebGIS is designed to be accessible to everyone, ensuring that users can gain valuable insights without the need for specialized technical knowledge.
